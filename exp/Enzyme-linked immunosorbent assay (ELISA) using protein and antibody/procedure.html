Implement of Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) using protein and antibody

Procedure :

  1. Direct ELISA: This is the simplest ELISA technique in which the antigen present in the sample is immobilized in the wells of a microtiter plate. The wells are then wash thoroughly removing all the other unadsorbed antigen. An enzyme linked antibody that is complementary to the antigen of interest is added to the wells and incubated for some time. The well is again washed so that only the bound antigen-antibody complex are left on the surface of the well. An substrate is then added which is converted into a detectable product by the enzyme linked antibody. Detection may be based on color, luminescence or fluorescence. Though this technique is much simpler and quicker than the other ELISA methods, there are a few disadvantages:

    • Conjugating the antibody with an enzyme might reduce the affinity of the antibody to the antigen, thus reducing the sensitivity of the reaction.
    • The complex samples containing a range of different antigens will get adsorbed onto the well along the antigen of interest. This will prove problematic when the antigen of interest is in low abundance as it will reduce the sensitivity of the test.


  2. Indirect ELISA: In this method, the sample containing antigen is adsorbed onto the microtiter well. A complimentary antibody is then added forming a antigen-antibody complex. Any unbound antibody is then washed away. The presence of the antibody bound to the antigen is then detected by adding an enzyme linked secondary antibody. This secondary antibody is raised against the primary antibody. Any unbound secondary antibody is then washed away and then the substrate for the enzyme is added. The amount of the colored reaction products thus formed is measured by a specialized spectrophotometric plate readers that can measured the absorbance of all the wells. Higher signal amplification is obtained in indirect ELISA as one or more secondary antibody can bind to primary antibody. However, cross reactivity from secondary antibody might perturb the purpose of the assay.

  3. Sandwich ELISA: In this ELISA, the antibody is immobilized instead of the antigen. These immobilized antibodies act as captured antibodies which are able to bind to specific antigen present in the sample with high affinity. A separate enzyme linked antibody is then used to detect the bound antigen as it recognizes a different epitope from that recognize by the immobilized first antibody. Thus, in this technique two separate antigen specific antibody are required and these antibodies need to bind to different epitopes of the same antigen. Any free secondary antibodies are washed away and a substrate for the enzyme is added. The amount of the colored reaction products is measured. Though this technique is highly flexible and sensitive, it is sometime difficult to find two different antibodies that recognize different epitopes on the antigen of interest and cooperate well in a sandwich format

  4. Competitive ELISA: Competitive ELISA is the most complex of all the known ELISA techniques. It is also known as inhibition ELISA because of the fact that it uses an inhibitor antigen. In this ELISA, the sample containing the antigen of interest is first incubated with an unlabelled primary antibody that leads to the formation of antigen-antibody complex. Since the antibody is used in excessive amount compared to the antigen there are free antibodies left. In the next step the antigen-antibody mixture is added to the plate coated with the inhibitor antigen. This inhibitor antigen can also bind with the primary antibody. The free antibody molecules are the only ones capable of binding to the inhibitor antigen while the antigen-antibody complexes do not bind and therefore washed off. The enzyme labelled secondary antibody is then added to the plate which binds the primary antibody bound to the inhibitor antigen. Finally, a substrate is added to react with the enzyme to emit a quantifiable signal for detection. The final signal in this procedure in inversely associated with the amount of antigen present in the sample. This means more the antigen present in the sample, the weaker will be the final signal.
